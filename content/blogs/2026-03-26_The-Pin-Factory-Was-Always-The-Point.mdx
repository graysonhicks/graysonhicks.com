## A Room Full of Half-Jobs

In the late 1700s, Adam Smith visited a pin factory.

It wasn't impressive at first glance. Just a few workers, some wire, and a repetitive process turning out something as trivial as pins.

But what caught his attention wasn't the product, it was how the work had been divided.

No one in the room was actually making a pin.

![A pin factory, circa late 1700s](/images/blog/pin-factory.png)

One man pulled wire. Another straightened it. Another cut it. Someone else sharpened the ends, someone formed the heads, another attached them, and others finished and packed. If you isolated any one of them, their contribution looked almost useless, just a fragment of a thing.

And yet, together, they were producing tens of thousands of pins a day.

Smith compares this to a man working alone. That man might make twenty pins, not because he's lazy or incapable, but because he's constantly switching between steps, constantly reorienting himself.

That's really the observation.

The difference wasn't talent. It was that no one had to carry the entire process at once.

---

## Where the Intelligence Actually Lives

The division wasn't arbitrary. Each step was small, but more importantly, it was stable and easy to evaluate. You could look at the output of a single step and know whether it was right without having to understand everything that came before or after.

Over time, the intelligence stops living in the worker and starts living in how the steps connect.

That principle shows up everywhere systems get built, from assembly lines to software pipelines. And it turns out it maps almost directly onto one of the harder problems in AI right now: getting multiple AI agents to work together reliably. You don't really appreciate how much the division matters until you try to build a multi-agent system without it.

---

## One Agent Doing Everything

Most people building multi-agent systems don't start that way.

You take a real problem and hand the whole thing to a single agent: analyze this dataset, write the report, make a decision, format the output.

For a while, it works. It even feels like you've found a shortcut, skipping all the intermediate structure and going straight from input to answer.

We had a system like this early on. One agent doing everything. It looked great in demos. Then we tried to make the output consistent across slightly different inputs.

That's where things started to drift.

Not in obvious ways. The format would shift a little, the emphasis would move, sometimes it would skip a step it had handled correctly the run before. Nothing was clearly broken, which made it harder to diagnose.

So we did what everyone does. Tightened the prompt. Added rules. Then added rules for when those rules didn't apply. It would improve for a bit, then something else would slip.

At some point it stopped feeling like engineering and started feeling more like negotiation.

The issue wasn't that the model couldn't reason. It was that we were overloading it, the same way Smith's lone pin-maker was overloaded. Not from lack of skill, but from carrying every step at once. These systems don't carry a stable internal state; they rebuild it from context every time, and that reconstruction depends heavily on how the input is shaped, what's nearby, and what gets emphasized. Every additional responsibility is another thing the model has to reorient itself around mid-stream, another chance for something to slip, just like the worker constantly switching between drawing wire, cutting, sharpening, and attaching heads.

That inconsistency is the problem.

## One Transformation at a Time

The pin factory gives you a useful constraint.

Instead of asking for the whole outcome, you ask for one transformation at a time.

If one step is just extraction, you can force it into a schema, validate it, retry it, or discard it entirely without affecting anything else. If the next step is structuring, you're operating on something already controlled instead of asking the model to both understand and clean the data at once.

By the time you get to the part that actually requires judgment, it's working on something that's already been shaped into a predictable form.

None of those individual steps are particularly impressive on their own, which can feel a little unsatisfying at first.

![The Pin Factory vs. A Multi-Agent System](/images/blog/pin-factory-multi-agent.png)

But once you start chaining them together, you have a multi-agent system. It stops behaving like a single pass and starts behaving like a pipeline. You can route based on outputs, reject results that don't meet a threshold, loop steps until they pass, or branch the process entirely.

That's usually what people are getting at when they say "agentic," but it only really works if the underlying steps are already separated. Otherwise you're just layering more responsibility onto the same overloaded process.

---

## Knowing When to Split

The hard part isn't the separation itself. It's knowing when a single agent is carrying too much, and when to hand part of the job to a new one.

There's no formula for this. It's a judgment call, the same kind a factory manager makes when they notice one station is bottlenecking the line. You watch for the signs. Output quality getting inconsistent. A prompt growing longer and more conditional. One part of the task interfering with another. Those are the moments where a new agent earns its place.

The temptation is to split too early or too late. Too early, and you're managing coordination overhead for something a single agent handles fine. Too late, and you've already spent weeks patching a prompt that needed to be two prompts.

The best multi-agent systems we've built didn't start with a clean architecture diagram. They started with one agent, and we paid attention to where it struggled. Each new agent was a response to a specific failure, not a guess about where complexity might show up later.

---

## Building Systems You Can See Into

Separation changes what you can see.

When everything happens inside one agent, a failure could be anywhere. The input might have been messy, the interpretation might have been off, the formatting might have drifted. You're left guessing, because the whole process is one opaque pass.

Once the steps are separated, the boundaries give you something to look at. You can log what comes out of each step, test one part without touching the others, and improve a single piece while watching how it affects the rest of the system.

Tools narrow in the same way. Instead of one agent deciding what to call from a wide-open set, each step only has access to what it actually needs. The retrieval step handles search, the calculation step handles computation, the formatting step enforces structure. Less surface area, fewer mistakes.

---

## The Model Worth Carrying

If you're building multi-agent systems, the pin factory is the mental model worth carrying.

Not because it's a perfect analogy, but because it keeps you honest about where the gains actually come from. The instinct, when things start to break, is to make the agent better. Add more instructions. Cover more cases. Make it "smarter." That's exactly what the pin factory worked against. The gains didn't come from improving the worker. They came from reducing what each worker had to do, and making the connections between them precise.

The moment you find yourself writing a long prompt that tries to do multiple things at once, enforce structure, reason, format, handle edge cases, you're fighting the same battle Smith's lone pin-maker was fighting. You can keep pushing in that direction. Most people do. Or you can stop and rearrange the work.

Smith was writing about pins, but he was really describing how complex things get made without relying on any single point of intelligence. Multi-agent systems work the same way. The smaller-step systems don't feel as impressive. They just keep working.
