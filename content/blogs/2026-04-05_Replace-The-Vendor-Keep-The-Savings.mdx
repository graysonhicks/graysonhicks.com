## The $250K Software Line Item Nobody Wants to Defend

In a lot of mid-size companies, there's a software contract that makes someone flinch every budget season.

It might be a $250,000 logistics platform built for companies ten times larger. Or a reporting suite with forty dashboards, three of which anyone actually uses. Or a CRM that took six months of consulting just to become tolerable.

The software works. It's fine. But it was never really built for them, and everyone knows it.

For years, the replacement math was simple: as expensive as the vendor was, going custom usually looked worse. Building software meant hiring a team, managing a project, and then owning the maintenance burden indefinitely. So the contract renewed. Then it renewed again.

That math is starting to change.

---

## Why the build-vs-buy equation is shifting

A few technical changes are compressing the cost of custom software.

**Code generation has gotten genuinely useful.** Not in the old sense of "maybe it helps with a function or two." In the much more practical sense of scaffolding working modules, admin tools, database layers, and backend logic from a clear description. It still needs a human in the loop. It still produces awkward code sometimes. But getting from zero to a usable first version is much cheaper than it was.

**Integration work is less painful now.** A lot of custom projects used to bog down in the middle: Stripe, Salesforce, warehouse systems, internal databases, old APIs with strange docs. That work still matters, but AI tools are much better at chewing through it. What used to take weeks of unglamorous implementation is often one of the faster parts of the build now.

**Maintenance is more manageable for a certain class of software.** Not every product, obviously. But for workflow-heavy internal tools, reporting systems, scheduling software, approval chains, and similar systems, the maintenance burden is more predictable than it used to be. Bug fixes, dependency updates, schema changes, small feature requests—those are exactly the kinds of bounded tasks AI assistance handles fairly well.

None of this makes custom software free. It just lowers the floor enough that some vendor contracts no longer look inevitable.

---

## A pricing model that fits: gainsharing

If a developer can replace an overpriced vendor platform for a fraction of the cost, the next question is how to price the work.

One model that fits neatly here is **gainsharing**: instead of charging a fixed fee, the developer takes a percentage of the savings they create.

Say a company is spending $250,000 a year on a vendor platform. A developer builds a replacement that costs $40,000 a year to maintain. That creates $210,000 in annual savings. Under a gainsharing agreement, the developer might take 30% of that savings for three years, or about $63,000 a year.

The company's spend drops immediately, from $250,000 to roughly $103,000 during the term. The developer earns well for building and maintaining software that is still far cheaper than the original contract. Both sides win, at least if the scope is real and the software holds up.

Gainsharing isn't new. The basic logic shows up in older incentive systems like the **Scanlon Plan**, which shared productivity gains between workers and management. Variants of the idea have appeared for decades in manufacturing, energy efficiency, supply chain work, and procurement. The principle is simple: price around value created, not just hours worked.

What's new is that software delivery has become cheap enough, in some cases, for that model to make sense.

## What makes a good target

Not every vendor contract is worth replacing. The good targets tend to have a few things in common.

**High cost relative to actual usage.** The company is paying enterprise pricing for a small subset of the platform.

**Bounded complexity.** The software supports an important workflow, but not one that depends on extreme technical sophistication.

**Stable requirements.** The workflow hasn't changed much in years, even if the vendor keeps shipping new features.

**Accessible data.** Migration is possible without getting trapped in a proprietary maze.

That usually points toward a certain kind of software: inventory tracking, approvals, client reporting, scheduling, document-heavy workflows, internal dashboards. Important enough to matter. Boring enough to be replaceable.

This is where domain knowledge starts to matter more than raw coding speed. A developer who understands logistics, construction, healthcare administration, legal ops, or manufacturing back offices can often see the replacement opportunity much more clearly than someone who only sees "software."

The moat usually isn't the code. It's knowing what can be safely left out.

## Why this matters for small shops

This looks especially promising for independent developers and small firms.

The opportunity is not to go after Salesforce or Oracle directly. It's to find the company paying enterprise prices for software that overshoots their actual needs by 80%. The software is too big, too generic, too expensive, and too embedded in habit to get questioned often.

AI lowers the cost of building, but it also lowers the cost of specializing. A small shop can go deep on one narrow vertical and make the next engagement faster, sharper, and easier to scope. Over time, that starts to look less like freelance development and more like a focused business.

And gainsharing changes the pitch. Instead of asking a client to gamble on a large custom build, you're saying something closer to: if the savings aren't real, this deal doesn't work.

That tends to get attention.

---

## The risk worth naming

The obvious risk is maintenance.

Vendor software carries a lot of hidden edge-case handling. Some of it is useless bloat. Some of it is scar tissue from years of production problems. A custom replacement can feel elegant in month one and become messy by month eighteen if nobody accounted for compliance changes, upstream API shifts, or the steady drip of "can it also do this?"

Gainsharing helps a little because the builder has an incentive to stay involved and keep the system healthy. But the agreement still has to be scoped carefully. What counts as maintenance? What counts as a change request? What happens at handoff? Who owns the code? What happens if a key integration changes upstream?

If those questions are vague, the model gets painful fast.

---

## What you can't really rebuild

Some products are expensive for reasons that have nothing to do with workflow software.

If the vendor's value comes from proprietary data, licensed feeds, patented methods, or models trained on data nobody else has, a custom rebuild won't be equivalent. Bloomberg is the standard example. You can rebuild parts of the interface. You cannot casually rebuild the data underneath it.

That's the filter worth using early: is the company paying for what the software does, or for what the software knows?

If it's the second one, the vendor may be earning its keep.

---

## The window

There's a window right now where the tools are good enough to make this model viable, but a lot of the market still hasn't adjusted. Companies are still renewing bloated contracts out of inertia. Developers are still defaulting to hourly billing or fixed bids when they could be pricing around measurable savings instead.

That gap creates an opening.

The people who do well here probably won't be the ones writing the most code. They'll be the ones who get good at spotting overpriced software, figuring out what actually matters in the workflow, and structuring deals around savings that are real enough to survive scrutiny.

That's a better business than generic custom development. And for a certain kind of developer, it's starting to look like a very good one.
