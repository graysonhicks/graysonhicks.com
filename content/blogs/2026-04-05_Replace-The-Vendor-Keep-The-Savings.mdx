## The $250K Line Item Nobody Questions

Somewhere in every mid-size company's budget, there's a software contract that makes someone wince during annual review. A $250,000-a-year platform that the team half-uses. Maybe it's a logistics tool built for an industry ten times their size. Maybe it's a reporting suite where they touch three of forty dashboards. Maybe it's a CRM that required six months of consulting just to configure.

The tool works. It's fine. But it was never built for them, and everyone quietly knows it.

Until recently, the math on replacing it didn't pencil out. Custom software meant hiring a team, managing a build, and maintaining something indefinitely. Even if the vendor's tool was bloated and overpriced, the cost of going custom was worse. So the contract renewed, year after year.

That math is changing.

---

## Why the Build-vs-Buy Equation Is Shifting

Three technical shifts are compressing the cost of building and maintaining custom software:

**Code generation has gotten genuinely useful.** Not useful in the "writes a function if you describe it perfectly" sense from a few years ago. Useful in the "scaffolds an entire working module from a description of what it should do" sense. AI coding tools can now generate full application layers — APIs, database schemas, admin interfaces — in hours instead of weeks. The output still needs a human reviewing and shaping it, but the baseline cost of getting working software from zero to one has dropped dramatically.

**Integration is no longer the hard part.** Connecting to Stripe, Salesforce, a warehouse API, an internal database — these used to be the unglamorous weeks of any custom build. Now an AI agent can read an API's documentation, generate the integration code, and write the tests. What used to be the slow, expensive middle of a project is becoming one of the faster parts.

**Maintenance is more predictable.** The ongoing cost of keeping software running — fixing edge cases, updating dependencies, adapting to changing requirements — has historically been the reason custom builds lose to vendors long-term. But AI-assisted maintenance is changing the ratio. Diagnosing a bug, writing a patch, updating a schema — these are exactly the kinds of scoped, well-defined tasks that AI tools handle well. A single developer with good tooling can now maintain what used to require a small team.

None of this means building custom software is free. It means the floor has dropped far enough that for a specific class of problem — replacing an expensive, underutilized vendor tool with something purpose-built — the economics are starting to favor the builder.

## Gainsharing: A Pricing Model for This Moment

So if a developer can build a replacement for a fraction of the vendor's annual cost, how should they charge for it?

There's a pricing model that fits naturally here, and it has a name: **gainsharing**. The idea is straightforward — instead of charging a fixed fee for the work, the developer takes a percentage of the savings they create.

Say a company spends $250,000 a year on a vendor platform. A developer proposes building a custom replacement that costs $40,000 a year to maintain. That's $210,000 in annual savings. Under a gainsharing agreement, the developer might take 30% of those savings for the first three years — roughly $63,000 a year. After three years, the company keeps the full savings and can either maintain the software themselves or negotiate a new maintenance agreement.

The company's software bill drops from $250,000 to about $103,000 immediately. The developer earns $189,000 over three years for building and maintaining something well within their capability. Both sides come out ahead.

Gainsharing isn't new. It has roots in manufacturing — the [Scanlon Plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanlon_plan) from the 1940s shared productivity gains between factory workers and management. It's been used in supply chain management, energy efficiency contracting, and government procurement for decades. The core principle is always the same: align incentives by splitting the value created rather than arguing over the cost of inputs.

What's new is that AI has made it viable for software. The build cost is finally low enough that the savings are real, measurable, and large enough to split.

## What Makes a Good Target

Not every vendor contract is worth replacing. The best candidates share a few traits:

**High cost relative to usage.** The company is paying enterprise pricing for a fraction of the platform's capability. They're subsidizing features they'll never touch.

**Domain-specific but not deeply complex.** The software does something important but bounded — inventory tracking, internal approvals, client reporting, scheduling. Not real-time trading systems or medical imaging.

**Stable requirements.** The core workflow hasn't changed much in years. The vendor keeps shipping features, but the company keeps using it the same way. This means the custom replacement won't need constant reinvention.

**Clear data ownership.** The company's data isn't locked inside the vendor's proprietary format in a way that makes migration impractical.

A developer who understands a specific industry — construction, logistics, healthcare administration, legal ops — can learn to spot these opportunities reliably. The domain expertise is the moat, not the code.

---

## The Niche Play

This is where it gets interesting for independent developers and small shops. The strategy isn't to compete with Salesforce head-on. It's to find the ten-person logistics company paying $200,000 a year for software that does 80% more than they need, and build them the 20% they actually use.

AI doesn't just lower the cost of building — it lowers the cost of *specializing*. A single developer can now credibly serve a narrow vertical: dental practices replacing their practice management platform, small manufacturers replacing their MES, regional banks replacing a vendor's loan origination workflow. Each engagement builds domain knowledge that makes the next one faster and more defensible.

The gainsharing model makes the pitch easy. The developer isn't asking the company to take a risk on a speculative build. They're saying: *if I don't save you money, I don't get paid.* That changes the conversation from "how much does custom software cost?" to "how much are you currently overpaying?"

---

## The Risk Worth Naming

The obvious risk is underestimating the maintenance burden. Vendor software, for all its bloat, handles a long tail of edge cases that only surface over time. A custom replacement that works beautifully in month one can become a headache by month eighteen if the developer didn't account for compliance updates, API changes upstream, or the slow accumulation of "can it also do this?" requests.

Gainsharing mitigates this somewhat — the developer stays involved for the contract period and has a financial incentive to keep things running. But scoping the agreement well matters. Clear boundaries on what's included, what counts as a change request, and what the handoff looks like at the end of the term are the difference between a good deal and a painful one.

## What You Can't Rebuild

Not every vendor product is just workflow logic wrapped in a subscription. Some are valuable precisely because of what's behind them — and no amount of AI tooling changes that.

**Proprietary data sources.** Some platforms are worth the price because they sit on top of data you can't get anywhere else. Bloomberg doesn't charge what it charges because the terminal is hard to build. It charges what it charges because it has decades of normalized financial data, proprietary indices, and real-time feeds from exchanges that aren't available through public APIs. You can build a dashboard that looks like Bloomberg. You can't build the data underneath it.

**IP-protected algorithms.** Other tools derive their value from patented or trade-secret methodology. A credit scoring platform might use a proprietary risk model trained on data you'll never have access to. A materials science simulation tool might rely on algorithms developed over years of R&D and protected by patents. The interface is just the delivery mechanism — the intellectual property is the product.

In both cases, the vendor's moat isn't the software. It's what the software knows or can do that you can't replicate. These are poor gainsharing candidates, not because the build would be hard, but because the replacement would be hollow. If the value lives in the vendor's data or IP, a custom frontend on top of commodity inputs won't deliver the same results.

The key question before scoping any replacement: *is the company paying for what the software does, or for what the software knows?* If it's the latter, the vendor contract is probably earning its keep.

---

## The Window

There's a window right now where the tools are good enough to make this viable but the market hasn't fully adjusted. Most companies are still renewing vendor contracts on autopilot. Most developers are still thinking in terms of hourly rates or fixed-bid projects.

The ones who figure out gainsharing — who learn to identify overpriced vendor contracts, scope the replacement accurately, and price the engagement around value delivered — are going to build very good businesses. Not by writing more code, but by writing the right code for people who are paying too much for the wrong software.
