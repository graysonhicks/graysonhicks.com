There's a mistake I keep seeing in how teams talk about AI agents.

They either talk about them like they're magic, or they talk about them like they're useless. Neither matches reality.

A better mental model: **AI agents are interns.**

That's not a knock. Good interns are useful — sometimes very useful. They save time, take work off your plate, and occasionally surprise you. But you don't let an intern rewrite your pricing strategy, handle an angry enterprise customer alone, or push mystery code to production on a Friday afternoon.

That's roughly where we are with agents. They can do work. They just can't carry the full weight of judgment, trust, and accountability that people keep trying to hand them.

## Why this mental model works

The confusion around agents usually comes from using the wrong frame:

- **Traditional software frame** — you expect deterministic behavior where there isn't any
- **Full employee frame** — you expect judgment, context, and consistency it doesn't have
- **Chatbot frame** — you underuse it

The intern model lands in a more useful place. Interns can handle real tasks — research, summarize, organize, draft, classify, follow a process. They need scoped assignments, context, and review. The quality of their output depends on how clearly the work was framed.

This framing also bridges the gap between technical and business teams:

- **Engineers** can think in terms of permissions, observability, guardrails, and rollback
- **Business leaders** can think in terms of delegation, supervision, training, and performance

Different vocabulary, same problem.

## Competency is real, but uneven

Two things are true at once: agents are capable, and agents are not broadly reliable. Same as interns.

**Where agents do well** — tasks with clear inputs, recognizable patterns, and visible success criteria:

- Summarizing meetings
- Pulling details out of support tickets
- Drafting internal documentation
- Writing code for narrow functions
- Routing requests
- Following checklists through known workflows

That's not toy work. In many companies, it's a meaningful chunk of the week.

**Where agents wobble** — work that's more _senior_ than _smart_:

- Tradeoffs under uncertainty
- Organizational context and politics
- Taste and timing
- Reading between the lines
- Knowing when the instruction is technically correct but wrong for the moment

The common failure pattern: a team sees a strong demo, quietly assigns the agent a level of judgment it hasn't earned, then is surprised when it behaves like a very fast junior.

## Delegation matters more than intelligence

Once you see agents as interns, the key question shifts from "Can this agent do the job?" to **"What can this agent do safely at this level of supervision?"**

That's better because delegation isn't about what something can do on a good day. It's about downside, reversibility, and review.

**A practical progression for agent delegation:**

1. **Assistance** — gather information, summarize, draft, transform, classify
2. **Proposal** — generate options, recommend next steps, prepare work for review
3. **Execution with guardrails** — take action in a narrow environment with permissions, logging, limits, and rollback
4. **Recurring operation** — spot-checked, not fully autonomous (few agents are here today)

Most agent use cases still belong in the first three modes. That's not a weakness — it's an honest read of where things are.

**The key insight:** if the task is fuzzy, the context is incomplete, and the review step is missing, the failure is in the delegation, not the output. A lot of AI disappointment is really management disappointment.

## Trust should be narrow and earned

This is where the intern model helps the most.

You don't trust an intern because they sound polished. You trust them because they've done a certain kind of work well, repeatedly, under known conditions. Even then, the trust stays local — prepping the weekly metrics deck doesn't mean negotiating a vendor contract.

**Agents should work the same way.** Trust should be:

- **Narrow** — scoped to specific tasks
- **Task-specific** — proven in this workflow, not assumed across workflows
- **Evidence-based** — built on repeated performance, not demos or confidence

**The common drift pattern:** a team sees a few good outputs and starts extending trust far beyond where it was earned:

- The triage agent starts making customer-facing decisions
- The internal scripting agent starts influencing architecture
- The copywriting agent operates with a brand voice nobody approved

That's how small mistakes become systemic ones.

**The bottom line:** trust is not a property of the model alone. It's a property of the model + the task + the context + the controls + the review process. An agent can be trustworthy for one workflow and completely untrustworthy for another.

## Accountability still belongs to humans

Output can be delegated. Accountability cannot.

If an intern writes a flawed memo and it goes out, the manager who approved it still owns the result. Same with agents.

This matters because agent output often:

- Arrives polished enough to _feel_ finished before it's been truly evaluated
- Moves through workflows fast enough to feel ownerless
- Looks detached from authorship

But somebody still owns the instructions, the permissions, the review threshold, and the business outcome when something goes wrong.

**Agents provide leverage, not accountability.** You can scale contribution before you can scale judgment — and those are not the same thing.

## Why engineers and business people talk past each other

Engineers and business leaders see different failure modes first:

| | **Engineers see** | **Business leaders see** |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Brittleness | Leverage |
| **Worry about** | Hallucinations, edge cases, silent failures, prompt drift | Overloaded teams, repetitive tasks, slow operations |
| **Fear** | Hidden unreliability | Leaving value on the table |

Both sides are usually noticing something real. The intern model gives them shared ground: the system is unreliable in ways normal software isn't, _and_ it can still create meaningful value, _and_ supervision is worth building when the offloaded work is substantial enough.

This is less a fight between believers and skeptics than a management problem:

- What work belongs where?
- What controls are proportional?
- What evidence is enough to widen the lane?

The technology is new. The management patterns aren't.

## Where the analogy breaks

Like any metaphor, this one has limits. Agents don't develop judgment like people do. They don't absorb context from hallway conversations. They don't care about outcomes.

**The critical difference is scale.** A mediocre intern creates a manageable amount of damage. A mediocre agent with broad access can create a lot of damage very quickly:

- Hundreds of bad messages
- Thousands of incorrect classifications
- A clean stream of plausible nonsense fed into downstream systems

The point isn't that agents _are_ interns. It's that organizations should manage them with intern-like expectations: **bounded responsibility, explicit oversight, measured trust, and human accountability.**

## This will change over time

"Agents are interns" won't stay true forever. In some narrow workflows, it already undersells what good systems can do. That surface area will expand — and it will expand from both directions.

**Models themselves will get better.** Each generation brings improved reasoning, longer context, fewer hallucinations, and better tool use. Tasks that require careful supervision today will become reliably autonomous tomorrow. The ceiling on what an agent can handle without hand-holding will keep rising — from intern-level work to analyst-level, and eventually to responsibilities that look more like a junior team member operating independently.

Some of that shift is already happening. Agents that could only draft are starting to evaluate. Agents that could only follow checklists are starting to handle branching decisions. The intern analogy is useful _now_, but the role agents fill will graduate over time just like a real hire would — except faster and across more domains simultaneously.

**The ecosystem around the models will also mature:**

- Stronger evals and benchmarks tied to real workflows
- Tighter guardrails and permission systems
- Better memory, monitoring, and audit trails
- Better process design for human-agent collaboration

Both matter. Raw model capability determines what an agent _can_ do. The surrounding systems determine what you can _trust_ it to do. Trust will grow the way it usually does in organizations: gradually, specifically, and after enough reps — but the pace will accelerate as both the models and the infrastructure improve together.

## The posture that seems right

For now, the healthiest posture is simple: **use agents, give them real work, let them create leverage — but manage them like interns.**

That means:

- **Scope assignments well** — clear inputs, defined outputs
- **Keep permissions tight** — least privilege, always
- **Review output proportional to risk** — not every task needs the same scrutiny
- **Measure in the workflow, not the demo** — real performance, not cherry-picked results
- **Expand trust slowly** — earned, not assumed
- **Keep accountability human** — no exceptions

This isn't anti-AI. It's probably the most practical way to get real value without drifting into hype or cynicism.

The teams that benefit most from agents won't be the ones who anthropomorphize them the hardest. They'll be the ones who learn to supervise them well.

And for now, that's what most agents need. A good manager.
