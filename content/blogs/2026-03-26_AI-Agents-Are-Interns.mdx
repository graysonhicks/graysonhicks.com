There's a mistake I keep seeing in how teams talk about AI agents.

They either talk about them like they're magic, or they talk about them like they're useless. Neither is right.

A better mental model: **AI agents are interns.**

That's not a knock. Good interns are useful, sometimes very useful. They save time, take work off your plate, and occasionally surprise you. But you don't let an intern rewrite your pricing strategy, handle an angry enterprise customer alone, or push mystery code to production on a Friday afternoon.

That's roughly where we are with agents. They can do real work. They just can't carry the judgment, trust, and accountability that people keep trying to hand them.

## Why this mental model works

Most confusion around agents comes from picking the wrong frame:

- **Traditional software frame**: you expect deterministic behavior where there isn't any
- **Full employee frame**: you expect judgment, context, and consistency it doesn't have

The intern model lands in a more useful place. Interns can handle real tasks: research, summarize, organize, draft, classify, follow a process. They need scoped assignments, context, and review. The quality of their output depends on how clearly the work was framed.

This frame also bridges technical and business teams:

- **Engineers** can think in terms of permissions, observability, guardrails, and rollback
- **Business leaders** can think in terms of delegation, supervision, training, and performance

Different vocabulary, but they're managing the same thing.

## Competency is real, but uneven

Agents are capable, and agents are not broadly reliable. Same as interns.

**Where agents do well**: tasks with clear inputs, recognizable patterns, and visible success criteria:

- Summarizing meetings
- Pulling details out of support tickets
- Drafting internal documentation
- Writing code for narrow functions
- Routing requests
- Following checklists through known workflows

That's not toy work. In most companies, it's a real chunk of the week.

**Where agents wobble**: work that's more _senior_ than _smart_:

- Tradeoffs under uncertainty
- Organizational context and politics
- Taste and timing
- Reading between the lines
- Knowing when the instruction is technically correct but wrong for the moment

The usual failure: a team sees a strong demo, quietly assigns the agent a level of judgment it hasn't earned, then acts surprised when it behaves like a very fast junior.

## Delegation matters more than intelligence

Once you see agents as interns, the question changes from "Can this agent do the job?" to **"What can this agent do safely at this level of supervision?"**

Delegation isn't about what something can do on a good day. It's about downside, reversibility, and review.

**A practical progression for agent delegation:**

1. **Assistance**: gather information, summarize, draft, transform, classify
2. **Proposal**: generate options, recommend next steps, prepare work for review
3. **Execution with guardrails**: take action in a narrow environment with permissions, logging, limits, and rollback
4. **Recurring operation**: spot-checked, not fully autonomous (few agents are here today)

Most agent use cases today fit in the first three modes.

If the task is fuzzy, the context is incomplete, and the review step is missing, the failure is in the delegation, not the output. Most AI disappointment is really management disappointment.

## Trust should be narrow and earned

You don't trust an intern because they sound polished. You trust them because they've done a specific kind of work well, repeatedly, under known conditions. Prepping the weekly metrics deck doesn't mean they negotiate the vendor contract.

Agents should work the same way. Trust should be **narrow, task-specific, and evidence-based**, not extrapolated from a few good demos.

I've watched this drift happen firsthand. A team sets up an agent to triage inbound support tickets, tagging priority, routing to the right queue. It does well for weeks. So someone extends it to draft the first response to customers. The drafts are decent, until a frustrated enterprise account gets a reply that's technically accurate but completely tone-deaf. The agent didn't know the customer had been escalated twice already. It didn't read the room because it doesn't have a room to read.

That's the pattern: trust earned in one lane gets quietly extended into another. The triage agent starts making customer-facing calls. The scripting agent starts influencing architecture. Small mistakes become systemic ones.

**Trust is not a property of the model alone.** It's a property of the model + the task + the context + the controls + the review process together.

## Accountability still belongs to humans

Output can be delegated. Accountability cannot.

If an intern writes a flawed memo and it goes out, the manager who approved it still owns the result. Same with agents.

This gets tricky because agent output often:

- Arrives polished enough to _feel_ finished before anyone's actually checked it
- Moves through workflows fast enough to feel ownerless
- Doesn't look like anyone wrote it

But somebody still owns the instructions, the permissions, the review threshold, and the business outcome when something goes wrong.

You can scale output before you can scale judgment. Those aren't the same thing.

## Why engineers and business people talk past each other

Engineers and business leaders see different failure modes first:

- **Engineers** focus on brittleness: hallucinations, edge cases, silent failures, prompt drift. They worry about hidden unreliability.
- **Business leaders** focus on leverage: overloaded teams, repetitive tasks, slow operations. They worry about leaving value on the table.

Both sides are usually right about what they're seeing. The intern model gives them shared ground: the system is unreliable in ways normal software isn't, _and_ it can still be genuinely useful, _and_ supervision is worth building when the offloaded work is big enough.

This is less a fight between believers and skeptics than a management problem:

- What work belongs where?
- What controls are proportional?
- What evidence is enough to widen the lane?

The technology is new. The questions are pretty ordinary.

## Where the analogy breaks

This metaphor has limits. Agents don't develop judgment like people do. They don't absorb context from hallway conversations. They don't care about outcomes.

The bigger difference is scale, and it cuts both ways. A good intern can handle one task at a time. A good agent can run hundreds of tasks in parallel, across time zones, without getting tired. That's genuine leverage no intern can match.

But a mediocre agent with broad access can make a huge mess just as fast:

- Hundreds of bad messages
- Thousands of incorrect classifications
- A clean stream of plausible nonsense fed into downstream systems

The upside and downside are both amplified. That's exactly why the intern-style controls (scoped responsibility, real oversight, trust that's earned rather than assumed) matter even more at scale, not less.

## This will change over time

"Agents are interns" won't stay true forever. In some narrow workflows, it already undersells what good systems can do.

**Models will keep getting better.** Each generation brings improved reasoning, longer context, fewer hallucinations, and better tool use. Agents that could only draft are starting to evaluate. Agents that could only follow checklists are starting to handle branching decisions. The ceiling will keep rising, from intern-level work to analyst-level, and eventually to something closer to a junior team member operating independently across real workflows.

The ecosystem around the models matters just as much: stronger evals, tighter permissions, better memory and monitoring, better process design for how humans and agents actually work together. What an agent can do is a model question. What you can trust it to do is a systems question.

The intern analogy is useful now. But the role agents fill will graduate over time, faster than a real hire and across more domains simultaneously.

## The posture that seems right

Use agents. Give them real work. But manage them like interns: scope the assignments, keep permissions tight, review output in proportion to risk, and expand trust only after they've earned it in _that specific lane_.

The teams that get the most out of agents won't be the ones who trust them fastest. They'll be the ones who learn to supervise them well. Right now, that's what most agents actually need. Not better prompts, not more autonomy. A good manager.
