Teams talk about AI agents like they're either magic or useless. Neither is right.

A better mental model: **AI agents are interns.**

That's not a knock. Good interns save time, take work off your plate, and occasionally surprise you. But you don't let an intern rewrite your pricing strategy, handle an angry enterprise customer alone, or push mystery code to production on a Friday afternoon.

That's where we are with agents. They can do real work. They just can't carry the judgment, trust, and accountability that people keep trying to hand them. _Shoutout to my former colleague [Kam](https://kamlasater.com/) for putting this idea in my head._

## Why this mental model works

Most confusion around agents comes from picking the wrong frame. Expect deterministic software and you'll be frustrated. Expect a full employee and you'll be disappointed.

The intern model lands in a more useful place. Interns handle real tasks: research, summarize, organize, draft, classify, follow a process. They need scoped assignments, context, and review. The quality of their output depends on how clearly the work was framed.

This frame also bridges technical and business teams. Engineers think in terms of permissions, guardrails, and rollback. Business leaders think in terms of delegation, supervision, and performance. Different vocabulary, same thing.

## Competency is real, but uneven

Agents are capable, and agents are not broadly reliable. Same as interns.

**Where agents do well**: tasks with clear inputs, recognizable patterns, and visible success criteria. Summarizing meetings. Pulling details from support tickets. Drafting documentation. Writing code for narrow functions. Routing requests. Following checklists. That's not toy work. In most companies, it's a real chunk of the week.

**Where agents wobble**: work that's more _senior_ than _smart_. Tradeoffs under uncertainty. Organizational context. Taste and timing. Knowing when the instruction is technically correct but wrong for the moment.

The usual failure: a team sees a strong demo, quietly assigns the agent a level of judgment it hasn't earned, then acts surprised when it behaves like a very fast junior.

## Delegation matters more than intelligence

Once you see agents as interns, the question changes from "Can this agent do the job?" to **"What can this agent do safely at this level of supervision?"**

Delegation isn't about what something can do on a good day. It's about downside, reversibility, and review.

**A practical progression:**

1. **Assistance**: gather information, summarize, draft, classify
2. **Proposal**: generate options, recommend next steps, prepare work for review
3. **Execution with guardrails**: take action with permissions, logging, limits, and rollback
4. **Recurring operation**: spot-checked, not fully autonomous (few agents are here today)

If the task is fuzzy, the context is incomplete, and the review step is missing, the failure is in the delegation, not the output.

## Trust should be narrow and earned

You don't trust an intern because they sound polished. You trust them because they've done a specific kind of work well, repeatedly, under known conditions.

Agents should work the same way. Trust should be **narrow, task-specific, and evidence-based**, not extrapolated from a few good demos.

I ran into this recently. A team built an underwriting agent and immediately expected it to do flawless financial math. When the numbers came back wrong, the reaction was disappointment. This thing can't even calculate a debt service coverage ratio? But LLMs aren't calculators. They're pattern matchers. Once we gave the agent proper math tools and added formula-based validation, the actual underwriters were freed from the grunt work of building spreadsheets and could focus on reviewing and validating the output. The team had been expecting a senior underwriter. They didn't realize they had an intern. A useful one, once managed correctly.

That's the pattern: people assign a level of competence the agent hasn't demonstrated, then blame the technology when it falls short. **Trust is not a property of the model alone.** It's a property of the model + the task + the tools + the controls around it.

## Accountability still belongs to humans

Output can be delegated. Accountability cannot.

If an intern writes a flawed memo and it goes out, the manager who approved it still owns the result. Same with agents. This gets tricky because agent output arrives polished enough to _feel_ finished, moves fast enough to feel ownerless, and doesn't look like anyone wrote it.

But somebody still owns the instructions, the permissions, the review threshold, and the outcome when something goes wrong. You can scale output before you can scale judgment.

## Where the analogy breaks

This metaphor has limits. Agents don't develop judgment like people do. They don't absorb context from hallway conversations. They don't care about outcomes.

The bigger difference is scale. A good intern handles one task at a time. A good agent can run hundreds in parallel, across time zones, without getting tired. But a mediocre agent with broad access can make a huge mess just as fast. Hundreds of bad messages, thousands of incorrect classifications, plausible nonsense fed into downstream systems.

The upside and downside are both amplified. That's why intern-style controls matter even more at scale, not less.

## This will change over time

"Agents are interns" won't stay true forever. In some narrow workflows, it already undersells what good systems can do.

Models will keep getting better. Agents that could only draft are starting to evaluate. Agents that could only follow checklists are starting to handle branching decisions. The ceiling will keep rising.

But what an agent can do is a model question. What you can _trust_ it to do is a systems question: evals, permissions, monitoring, process design. The intern analogy is useful now. The role agents fill will graduate over time, faster than a real hire and across more domains simultaneously.

## The posture that seems right

Use agents. Give them real work. But manage them like interns: scope the assignments, keep permissions tight, review output in proportion to risk, and expand trust only after they've earned it in _that specific lane_.

The teams that get the most out of agents won't be the ones who trust them fastest. They'll be the ones who learn to supervise them well.
